The Causes of the French Revolution

Assignment
The French Revolution in 1789 did not come from nowhere. It was the result of many problems that had been building up for decades. In this essay I will explain the main causes of the French Revolution. The main causes are typically grouped into four categories: financial crisis, social inequality, Enlightenment ideas, and food shortages. Each of these mattered on its own, but the revolution happened because they all came together at the same time.
The most obvious problem was money. France was in serious debt by the 1780s. The government had spent enormous amounts supporting wars, especially the American Revolution, which alone cost about 1.3 billion livres. Supporting the Americans was popular with some French leaders because it hurt Britain, but it was expensive. King Louis XVI tried to raise new taxes to cover the deficit, but the nobility and clergy resisted paying. This forced him to call the Estates-General in 1789, which he had hoped to avoid.
The Estates-General is important because it is where the revolution really got started. It was a representative body with three sections: First Estate (clergy), Second Estate (nobility), and Third Estate (commoners). It had not been convened since 1614. Each estate got one vote, so the First and Second could outvote the Third even though the Third represented most of the country. When the Third Estate got frustrated they broke away and declared themselves the National Assembly. They swore the Tennis Court Oath, promising not to disband until France had a new constitution.
The social system was also deeply unfair. The First and Second Estates had special privileges. They paid almost no taxes. They held the best government jobs. They owned most of the land. Meanwhile, the Third Estate was 97 percent of the population and paid almost all the taxes. Inside the Third Estate there were also divisions. Wealthy merchants and professionals, called the bourgeoisie, wanted political rights. Peasants and urban workers wanted basic things like food and lower rents. This meant the Third Estate was not really one group, but a lot of different groups with different complaints, all of them angry at the system.
New ideas from Enlightenment writers made people question whether things had to be this way. That is a real debate among historians. Some argue material conditions (hunger, debt) were the main trigger, while Enlightenment ideas gave people a framework to understand their grievances. Writers like Rousseau and Voltaire made the old order seem illegitimate, not just unjust. Rousseau's The Social Contract argued that legitimate government comes from the consent of the governed. Montesquieu wrote about separating powers so no one person could dominate. These ideas had spread widely in France by 1789. Educated people had read them, and even those who hadn't read them directly heard about the ideas from others. Some historians think the Enlightenment was the most important cause because it gave people a way to imagine something different from what they had.
Food shortages were probably the most immediate cause. Poor harvests in 1788 caused bread prices to spike. Bread was the main food for most French people, sometimes making up 50% of their income. The winter of 1788-89 was unusually harsh, making things worse. Hungry peasants and urban workers were primed for unrest. People blamed the government for not fixing the problem. Hungry people are angry people, and angry people are more willing to take action. The Women's March on Versailles in October 1789 was partly about bread. The women literally demanded that the king come back to Paris where bread was more available.
What is interesting about 1789 is how fast everything happened once it started. May — Estates-General meets. June — Third Estate declares itself the National Assembly (Tennis Court Oath). July 14 — Storming of the Bastille. August — Declaration of the Rights of Man. October — Women's March on Versailles brings the king back to Paris. In less than six months, everything had changed.
It would be wrong to say one cause was the real reason for the revolution. I think the financial crisis forced King Louis XVI to call the Estates-General, which gave the Third Estate a chance to demand reform. The social system made them furious enough to demand big changes instead of small ones. Enlightenment ideas gave them the language and arguments to make those demands. And the food shortages gave the common people, who normally had no political power, a reason to get involved in the streets.
I also want to add something the textbooks sometimes skip over. Women played a major role from the beginning. The Women's March on Versailles was probably the most dramatic event of October 1789, and it was led by women who were tired of being unable to feed their families. During the revolution, writers like Olympe de Gouges wrote a Declaration of the Rights of Woman to argue that the revolution's promises should include women. She was eventually executed, but her work showed that the revolution was contested from inside, not just from supporters of the old order.
In conclusion, the French Revolution happened because many problems came together at once. No single cause would have been enough by itself. The money crisis forced a political opening. The unfair social system created deep anger. Enlightenment ideas gave people a new way to think about government. Food shortages turned that anger into action. The causes of the French Revolution remind us that revolutions don't come from one spark. They come from years of pressure until something breaks.
